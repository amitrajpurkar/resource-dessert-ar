What is a Resource Desert?

A Jacksonville ZIP code where essential services — healthcare, healthy food, parks, and insurance — are physically or financially out of reach for the people who need them most.

Three key questions driving this analysis:

  1. What is the biggest mismatch between residents and available essential services, broken down by ZIP code?
  2. How do resource deserts directly affect long-term health outcomes — specifically the link between preventative assets and poor outcomes?
  3. Where should we put the right resources to achieve the greatest impact for a specific Jacksonville community?

Methodology & Data

How We Built the Desert Score

We combined ten public datasets to create a composite Desert Score (0–100) for every Jacksonville ZIP code. Higher scores indicate greater deprivation.

Desert Score Formula

Desert Score = Demand Factor × ( w₁ × HealthcareGap + w₂ × FoodAccessGap + w₃ × ParksGap + w₄ × InsuranceGap )
Demand Factor = f(poverty rate, disease burden, social vulnerability)
Weights: w₁=0.35 (healthcare), w₂=0.30 (food), w₃=0.20 (parks), w₄=0.15 (insurance)

Health Outcomes

What Do Resource Deserts Do to Health?

We tested whether ZIP codes with fewer preventative assets also show worse health outcomes. The scatter plot below plots each Jacksonville ZIP on two dimensions: supply of preventative resources (x-axis) and rate of poor health outcomes (y-axis).

🔬

Strongest Correlation Found

Primary Care Access ↔ Poor Mental Health

ZIP codes with fewer primary care providers show significantly higher rates of poor mental health among adults. This relationship is the strongest across all asset–outcome pairs tested (Pearson r ≈ −0.72).
